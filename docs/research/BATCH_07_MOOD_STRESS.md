## Cross-check notes

- Emotional well-being was checked across CDC and NIMH. The Happiness page explicitly distinguishes well-being from constant cheerfulness and acknowledges biological, physical, relational, environmental, and social influences.
- Depression symptoms, course, chronic-illness links, and treatment categories were checked with NIMH. The page avoids diagnosis from one symptom and includes medical, medicine, sleep, substance, and bipolar-spectrum considerations.
- Anxiety wording was checked across NIMH anxiety resources. The page distinguishes temporary anxiety from disorders and directs acute cardiac, neurologic, respiratory, and glucose symptoms to medical assessment rather than assuming panic.
- Stress wording was checked across CDC and NIMH. The page distinguishes an identifiable stress response from persistent anxiety while acknowledging that the two may overlap.
- Self-care wording was checked against NIMH. Sleep, meals, hydration, movement, relaxation, realistic goals, and social connection are presented as supportive foundations, not as cures or substitutes for treatment.
- Social-connection wording was checked with CDC. The page emphasizes relationship quality, boundaries, abuse and coercive-control concerns, disability, transport, culture, language, money, privacy, and digital access.
- Relaxation wording was checked with NCCIH. The page includes slow-breathing and mindfulness cautions for panic, dizziness, trauma symptoms, dissociation, air hunger, and worsening distress.
- Suicide-warning wording was checked with NIMH. Because the app is intended to remain offline and not tied to one country, patient-facing wording refers to local emergency or crisis services. Exact contacts and response steps remain clinical-review-required.
- Medication and substance wording is deliberately non-prescriptive. The app never directs a psychiatric medicine start, stop, taper, dose change, or abrupt substance cessation.
